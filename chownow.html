ChowNow







I designed ChowNow as a final project for my COGS 120 Interaction Design class. In teams of 3, we designed a mobile app that helps users decide on a place to eat via minigame. We wanted to simplify the decision process behind deciding where to eat.
My role:

- research (observations, interviews)
- storyboarding
- wireframes
- prototyping (wireframes, paper prototyping, testing)








Phase 1: Ideation








Storyboarding



My team immediately gravitated towards the problem space of food, due to our class being right before lunchtime. Everyone in the team made an individual storyboard tackling food-related needs. I made a quick storyboard where a group of friends used an app to easily choose a dinner location. Other storyboards involved grocery shopping financial planners, and food deliveries.


After discussing which idea to run with, we decided to go with my idea of using an app to easily choose a dinner location. We recognized that Yelp already provided a lot of useful services in helping people find a place to eat: large database, user reviews, search filtering, and more. However, Yelp ultimately left the choice up to the user. We wanted to present users with a choice that they could accept or reject. My team felt that by presenting users with a choice, group decisions on where to eat would be resolved much more quickly.







Phase 2: Prototyping













Paper Prototype



We made a paper prototype to flesh out the flow and layout of the app. The paper prototype was very basic, and actions remained limited, with the "Explore" feature being search filtering & the "Recent" feature being a log that kept track of previous choices.


As expected, the main feedback from our peers on the prototype centered on how Yelp already provided these services. Furthermore, the prototype did not have a mechanism that would present a choice to the user. We struggled to figure out how to achieve this. We needed user input in order to understand what kind of food options users enjoyed, but we wanted to take the stress and complexity of deciding where to eat. Stymied by this, we moved on to the next step: wireframing.



Wireframes







We made wireframes via Google Slides to further flesh out the layout of the app. While comparing color schemes, our group made the realization that we could use the simple, yet useful mechanism of comparison to guide user decision-making process. We pivoted and implemented a mini-game that would make users pick between 2 options, with the chosen option remaining and the other option being replaced next round by another option. By doing this, we would get user input while only asking users to simply compare 2 options.



Functional Prototype







Next, I set out to make a functional prototype on Heroku. My teammates conducted A/B testing on the number of rounds to present to the user. We concluded that users want more than 3 options presented to them, but not more than 6. This was measured through by the number of clicks on the "No, Play Again" button. The rest of the app's features remained the same, with the "Explore" feature launching the comparison minigame, "Settings" to apply filters, and "Recent Activity" detailing past choices.







Project Submission



We made a poster to present our design process as we pitched to judges how our app would speed up deciding where to eat via our minigame solution. Judges were interested in how we came across that solution, and I gained immense experience with pitching a product while learning valuable skills on how to draw viewers in and keep their attention. I also enjoyed seeing other students' projects, which tackled a variety of other problem spaces. In the future, I plan to go to more design showcases, so that I can expand my design vision to one beyond that of a student.
